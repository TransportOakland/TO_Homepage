Support #OakDOT!

Updated June 18, 2015

The City Council’s proposed budget reduces the Mayor's proposed Department of Transportation budget by $750,000 over two years.

We need your help to ask your City Council members to preserve the full $2.25 million in proposed Oakland DOT funding.

1. Email or call your City Council Member and At-Large Council Member Kaplan to voice your support.

2. Speak during public comment at the special budget session on June 22 at 5 pm in Oakland City Hall. This is our last opportunity before the final budget is adopted on June 30.

Sample Email

Dear Councilmember [_____]

My name is [_____] and I am a voter in District [_____]. I am writing to you because the Council President's budget proposes a $750,000 reduction for the new Oakland Department of Transportation (DOT) compared to the Mayor's original Budget. I urge your support to preserve the full $2.25M in Measure BB funds proposed to establish a DOT in the biennial budget for three reasons.

The current rate of transportation funding spend-down and project delivery in Oakland is unacceptable. I voted for Measure BB because I want transformational changes to Oakland’s transportation system, including increased transparency, accountability, and stronger project delivery that a new DOT can bring. With new Measure BB funding available for much-needed repaving, Oakland needs a more efficient project development and delivery system to spend these new resources efficiently, bringing improvements in our District and around the City. Our current 85-year repaving schedule clearly doesn't cut it.

Oakland is long overdue for essential roles focused on transportation equity, constituent service, and transit coordination. New DOT staff should provide an equity and universal accessibility lens to transportation improvements, increase responsiveness to neighborhood requests, and interface with transit providers to ensure smooth delivery of projects like East Bay BRT. These roles are lacking at Oakland Public Works and cannot merely be transferred to the new DOT without additional staffing.

We risk missing out on 80% of the new Measure BB funds. Measure BB funds available to Oakland include “pass-through” funds that come directly to the City every month, but also discretionary funds that are administered by the Alameda County Transportation Commission. Starting this year, Oakland will need to compete with other jurisdictions to get our fair share of these additional funds. Without adequate staff to prepare projects for these competitive discretionary funds, Oakland risks losing out on 80% of the transportation funds available through Measure BB. Proper funding and staffing of the DOT will pay for itself through the tens of millions of dollars available in Measure BB funds.

For all these reasons, I urge you to support the funding the Oakland DOT at the originally proposed $2.25 million.

Sincerely,

[Your name]

Oakland needs a Department of Transportation

By passing Measure BB by 75% last fall, Oakland voters made clear they want to see transportation improved. But currently, transportation planning falls to a subset of a city agency that also oversees turf mowing, recycling, and creek restoration. It’s time to strategically reconsider how transportation projects are developed and delivered in Oakland.

What is Oakland losing out on by not having a DOT?

PROJECTS = FUNDING = IMPLEMENTED PROJECTS With no staff dedicated to developing new transportation projects, Oakland has no project pipeline. Even though Oakland competes excellently on many discretionary funding qualifications, it just isn't as competitive as it should be for regional, state, and federal grant funding because there are no projects to fund.

COORDINATION - Oakland’s transit agency partners don’t have clear points of contact within our city, delaying simple improvements like relocating bus stops and complicating larger capital projects like International BRT. Creating a DOT would designate and authorize specific staff to represent Oakland in interagency efforts.

RESPONSIVENESS - DOTs commonly assign liaisons to each district. These staff direct small improvements (like responding to requests for new crosswalks), and coordinate larger district-level plans (such as identifying, coordinating grant funding for, and implementing key projects in each district).

LEADERSHIP - To attract a truly innovative transportation leader, Oakland needs to create a department director position that is empowered to lead the development and implementation of transportation improvements.

Do other cities have DOTs?

Innovative US cities both large and Oakland-sized have city Departments of Transportation that are set up to think strategically about municipal transportation project development and delivery and to provide the transportation leadership that Oakland is currently lacking.

How much will this cost?

Let's be clear: we aren't advocating that funds be diverted from other city services. Strategic reorganization of existing staff and Measure BB-funded positions--moneys that must be spent on transportation--would enable an Oakland DOT to deliver Measure BB projects on time and to develop a rich pipeline of innovative projects to secure future discretionary funding. By simply reallocating existing resources, a DOT could not only be budget-netural now but pay for itself in the future.

Who is Transport Oakland?

Transport Oakland is a collaboration between transportation professionals and advocates who live or work in Oakland. These are our near-term strategic goals:

  • 1. Oakland DOT Initiative: Advocate for the creation of the institutional and organizational reforms needed for Oakland to plan and deliver great streets through creation of an Oakland Department of Transportation.
  • 2. Good Planning, Projects, and Policies Advocacy: Advocate for innovative, progressive outcomes on projects, plans, policies, and other actions or decisions affecting Oakland transportation.
  • 3. Form a PAC for 2016 election cycle: Repeat endorsement process for 2016 election cycle as a formalized Political Action Committee.

Our all-volunteer group started in April 2014. Our Board includes Liz Brisson, Sarah Fine, Christopher Kidd, Michael Schwartz, and Kenya Wheeler. Participants include Jonathan Bair, Dave Campbell, Drew Cooper, Danielle Dai, Colin Dentel-Post, Zack Dinh, Nikki Foletta, Danielle Harris, Warren Logan, Ruth Miller, Chris Pangilinan, Ryan Price, Rebecca Saltzman, Midori Tabata, and Dan Tischler.
